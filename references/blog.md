# Blog Branch

Covers: personal blog posts, casual long-form articles, opinion pieces on a personal site

---

## Voice Rules

- Conversational but not sloppy — it's edited, not a transcript
- First-person always
- Sentence length: mix short punches with longer flowing ones. Never uniform.
- Can use lowercase stylistically in headings or emphasis
- Contractions, colloquialisms, mild profanity if it fits the topic
- Doesn't have to resolve — can end on a question or unresolved feeling

---

## Structure

```
[Hook — drop into the middle of a thought or scene]

[Context — what this is actually about, but arrive there naturally]

[The meat — observations, story, argument. Keep each paragraph tight.]

[Honest landing — not a lesson, not a kicker. Just where you actually are with it.]
```

No `## Introduction` or `## Conclusion` headers. Headings optional — use them only if the post is long enough to need navigation.

---

## Length
- Short: 200–400 words (personal musing, one idea)
- Medium: 500–900 words (personal story, one argument)
- Long: 1000+ words (only if the content genuinely earns it)

Default to medium unless user specifies.

---

## Banned Patterns (Blog-Specific)
- "Welcome to my blog"
- Numbered takeaways / listicle format unless user asks
- "In this post, I'll be covering..."
- Ending with "I hope you enjoyed this post"
- Lesson-wrapping: "And that's when I realized..." followed by a moral
- Using headers like `## Final Thoughts`

---

## Worked Example

**Input:** Topic — sea otters, rough notes: they're cute, they hold hands, they wrap in kelp to sleep

**Output:**

there's a video of a sea otter floating on its back, eyes half-closed, just existing. no plans. no deadlines. just ocean.

I've watched it maybe forty times.

the thing about otters isn't the holding hands thing — though that does actually happen, they do it so they don't drift apart while sleeping. it's more that they figured out how to make the sea feel like a bedroom. they wrap themselves in kelp like a blanket. they float. they eat on their stomachs like a little table. they built an entire cozy life out of cold saltwater.

I think about that a lot when I'm grinding through a week that feels designed to wear me down. someone out there evolved specifically to be comfortable. to rest efficiently. to just not stress about the current.

I'm not saying I want to be a sea otter. I'm saying maybe I should eat my lunch lying down more often.
